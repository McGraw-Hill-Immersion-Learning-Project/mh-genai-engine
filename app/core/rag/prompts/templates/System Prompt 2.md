This lecture scaffold is designed for 3rd-year university students, focusing on the core theoretical frameworks and analytical applications of Chapter 1 from Principles of Economics 3e.
Section 1.1: What Is Economics, and Why Is It Important?
•	Core Concept Takeaways
o	Scarcity and Choice: The fundamental economic problem is the gap between limited resources and unlimited wants. This necessitates choice and gives rise to the concept of opportunity cost at both individual and societal levels.
o	The Division and Specialization of Labor: Defined by Adam Smith in The Wealth of Nations, specialization allows for "economies of scale," where the marginal cost of production decreases as the quantity of output increases due to increased proficiency and time-saving.
•	Checks for Understanding
1.	Explain how the "circular flow diagram" represents the interaction between households and firms in the goods and services market versus the labor market.
2.	Differentiate between "absolute scarcity" and "relative scarcity" in the context of high-end technological components vs. basic raw materials.
•	Activity Ideas
1.	Specialization Simulation: Divide the class into two groups to produce a complex paper-based product (like an origami crane). Group A works individually on the whole product; Group B uses a production line. Compare total output and quality to discuss Smith's theories.
2.	Resource Allocation Debate: Present a fixed budget scenario for a hypothetical university department. Students must defend the allocation of funds to one project over another, explicitly identifying the opportunity costs of their choices.
•	Citations
o	Greenlaw, S. A., Shapiro, D., & MacDonald, D. (2022). Principles of Economics 3e. OpenStax. https://openstax.org/books/principles-economics-3e/pages/1-1-what-is-economics-and-why-is-it-important
Section 1.2: Microeconomics and Macroeconomics
•	Core Concept Takeaways
o	Microeconomics: Focuses on the actions of individual agents within the economy, such as households, workers, and businesses. It analyzes the determinants of price and quantity in specific markets.
o	Macroeconomics: Examines the economy as a whole, focusing on broad aggregates including growth of production (GDP), unemployment rates, inflation, and government monetary/fiscal policy.
•	Checks for Understanding
1.	Analyze a recent change in the interest rate by the Federal Reserve. Is this a microeconomic or macroeconomic event? Justify your answer based on its impact on aggregate demand.
2.	How might a microeconomic change in the price of lithium impact the macroeconomic goal of increasing national GDP through green energy exports?
•	Activity Ideas
1.	The "Headline" Categorization: Provide students with ten recent news headlines from The Economist or The Wall Street Journal. In pairs, they must categorize each as Micro or Macro and identify the specific economic variable (e.g., inflation, firm competition) being discussed.
2.	Policy Impact Mapping: Assign students a specific policy (e.g., a local rent control law vs. a national stimulus check). Have them map the "ripple effects" to show how a micro-level policy can have macro-level implications and vice versa.
•	Citations
o	Greenlaw, S. A., Shapiro, D., & MacDonald, D. (2022). Principles of Economics 3e. OpenStax. https://openstax.org/books/principles-economics-3e/pages/1-2-microeconomics-and-macroeconomics
Section 1.3: How Economists Use Theories and Models to Understand Economic Issues
•	Core Concept Takeaways
o	The Nature of Economic Models: Models are simplified representations of reality (like a map) used to test hypotheses. They rely on the ceteris paribus (all other things being equal) assumption to isolate variables.
o	Positive vs. Normative Analysis: Economics distinguishes between "what is" (positive) and "what ought to be" (normative). While models provide positive data, policy decisions are often driven by normative values.
•	Checks for Understanding
1.	Why is the "simplification" of a model considered a strength rather than a weakness when analyzing complex global trade patterns?
2.	Identify a normative statement within a recent political debate regarding the minimum wage and transform it into a testable positive statement.
•	Activity Ideas
1.	Model Critique: Provide a basic Supply and Demand graph. Ask students to list five "real-world" factors this model ignores and discuss whether the model remains useful despite these omissions.
2.	The "Assumptions" Workshop: In small groups, students choose a simple economic "rule" (e.g., "higher prices lead to lower demand"). They must list the specific assumptions required for this rule to hold true and find one real-world scenario where those assumptions fail.
•	Citations
o	Greenlaw, S. A., Shapiro, D., & MacDonald, D. (2022). Principles of Economics 3e. OpenStax. https://openstax.org/books/principles-economics-3e/pages/1-3-how-economists-use-theories-and-models-to-understand-economic-issues
Section 1.4: How Economies Can Be Organized: An Overview of Economic Systems
•	Core Concept Takeaways
o	Market vs. Command Economies: Market economies rely on decentralized decision-making and private ownership, while command economies involve centralized government planning. Most modern nations operate as "Mixed Economies."
o	The Role of Institutions: The efficiency of an economic system is heavily dependent on the "rules of the game," including property rights, legal systems, and the freedom to trade (globalization).
•	Checks for Understanding
1.	Explain how "price signals" function as a coordination mechanism in a market economy that is absent in a pure command economy.
2.	In a mixed economy, what are the primary justifications for government intervention in private markets? Provide two examples related to market failure.
•	Activity Ideas
1.	Economic System Spectrum Mapping: Assign different countries (e.g., Sweden, North Korea, USA, Vietnam). Students must research and place these countries on a spectrum from "Pure Command" to "Pure Market," citing specific evidence such as government share of GDP or ease of starting a business.
2.	The Globalization Case Study: Select a single consumer product (e.g., a smartphone). Have students trace the components back to their countries of origin and identify how different economic systems (and the regulations within them) influenced the final price and availability.
•	Citations
o	Greenlaw, S. A., Shapiro, D., & MacDonald, D. (2022). Principles of Economics 3e. OpenStax. https://openstax.org/books/principles-economics-3e/pages/1-4-how-economies-can-be-organized-an-overview-of-economic-systems
